01 — Context

Meal planning is broken

Existing meal planning tools treat food as data — calorie counts, macro splits, rigid weekly grids. They ignore the reality of how people cook: imperfectly, improvisationally, with whatever's already in the fridge.

The result is an ecosystem of apps that generate beautiful plans nobody follows. Food waste persists. Grocery runs stay chaotic. And the cognitive load of "what's for dinner" remains one of the most surprisingly draining daily decisions.

Sauté was designed to close that gap — to build a system that adapts to the cook, not the other way around.

02 — Approach

Intelligence that defers to the cook

The core interaction model centers on a Smart Pantry — a living inventory of what the user actually has, built through a scan-your-fridge flow powered by GPT-4 Vision. Point the camera, confirm the detected items, and the system knows your starting point.

From there, Sauté generates meal suggestions that prioritize ingredients nearing expiry, thread leftovers across multiple days, and adapt to stated dietary preferences without being prescriptive. The AI layer is deliberately quiet — it surfaces possibilities rather than mandating plans.

One-tap checkout pipes the remaining ingredients directly into an Instacart cart. The entire flow — from scanning the fridge to a filled grocery cart — takes under two minutes.

The best interface for food is no interface at all — just the right suggestion, at the right moment, with the right ingredients already on hand.

03 — Research

Understanding real kitchen behavior

Fourteen in-depth interviews with home cooks across a spectrum of skill levels — from college students surviving on basics to seasoned weeknight optimizers. Contextual inquiry sessions in five kitchens. Two weeks of diary studies tracking actual meal decisions, not aspirational ones.

Three patterns emerged consistently: decision fatigue peaks around 4pm, the threshold for engaging with a planning app is brutally low (under 90 seconds), and the single biggest source of food waste is forgotten leftovers — not over-purchasing.

These findings directly shaped the product's three core pillars: pantry awareness, leftover threading, and frictionless checkout.

04 — System

A visual language for modern cooking

The identity balances warmth with precision. Deep surfaces, restrained typography, and generous whitespace create a canvas that lets food photography — and the user's own ingredients — occupy center stage.

The component system was built for density without clutter. Ingredient cards carry expiry indicators with a traffic-light color logic that communicates urgency without anxiety. Recipe tiles prioritize a single hero image and estimated time, nothing more.

Motion is minimal and purposeful — swipe-to-dismiss for suggestions, subtle parallax on pantry items, and a satisfying check-off animation when ingredients are added to the cart.

05 — Engineering

The architecture beneath the surface

The frontend is a React application with TypeScript, structured around a feature-module architecture. State management is minimal by design — React Context for global pantry state, local state for everything else. No Redux. No unnecessary abstraction layers.

The Vision AI pipeline processes fridge images server-side through a Node.js/Express backend. Images are compressed client-side before upload, analyzed via GPT-4 Vision with a structured prompt chain that first identifies items, then cross-references against a curated ingredient taxonomy for consistent naming.

Recipe generation uses a constrained prompt strategy — the model receives pantry contents, dietary flags, and a "use-first" priority list (expiring items), then returns structured JSON that the frontend renders without additional parsing. The entire AI layer is replaceable — deliberately abstracted behind service interfaces to survive model migrations.

06 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

The scan accuracy, while impressive in controlled environments, degrades meaningfully with cluttered fridges and poor lighting. A production version would need a confirmation-and-correction loop that's more forgiving than the current binary accept/reject model.

I'd also invest earlier in collaborative features. Cooking is social — households share kitchens, preferences conflict, and grocery responsibilities are rarely solo. The current architecture supports a single-user mental model. Extending it to households is the clear next step.
